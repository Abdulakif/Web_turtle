Sri lanka is a home to several fascinating species of turtles. The green turtle, hawksbill turtle, Olive Ridley, loggerhead turtle, and leatherback turtle the five principal types of turtles found in Sri Lanka. Every species has distinctive traits, nesting habits, and conservation requirements. These gorgeous organisms are essential to keep the ecological balance of marine environments. Sri Lanka works to save and conserve these turtles for present and future generations to enjoy and admire through conservation efforts and public education.

Green Turtle

The green turtle (Chelonia mydas), an endangered species of marine turtle, can grow to a length of four feet and a weight of 500 pounds. These turtles are so named because their herbivorous diet gives their fat a greenish tint. Unfortunately, they are threatened by things like poaching, pollution, and habitat damage. The protection of these exquisite species and their aquatic habitats depends on conservation initiatives.

Loggerhead Turtle

The threatened loggerhead turtle (Caretta caretta) is distinguished by its huge head and powerful jaws. Oceans all throughout the world are home to these turtles. Long-distance migrants, loggerhead turtles are known for building their nests on sand beaches. They struggle with issues like pollution, habitat degradation, and unintentional entanglement in fishing gear. The protection of loggerhead turtles and ensuring their survival in the waters depend heavily on conservation activities.

Hawksbill Turtle

The hawksbill turtle (Eretmochelys imbricata) is a critically endangered species of marine turtle known for its unusual, pointed beak and exquisitely patterned shell. All around the world, these turtles can be found in tropical waters and coral reefs. Due to their diet of sponges and other invertebrates, hawksbills are crucial for maintaining the ecosystems of coral reefs. However, they are seriously threatened by things like habitat loss, pollution, and the illegal trade in their shells. Hawksbill turtle protection and ocean biodiversity preservation both require immediate conservation action.

Olive Ridley turtle

The endangered olive ridley turtle (Lepidochelys olivacea) is distinguished by its olive-colored carapace. Olive ridleys engage in mass nesting activities known as arribadas in tropical and subtropical waters. Threats to them include egg harvesting, unintentional catch in fishing gear, and habitat loss. To safeguard olive ridley turtles and guarantee their longevity in the waters, conservation activities are essential.

Leatherback turtle

The largest species of sea turtle is the leatherback (Dermochelys coriacea), which is renowned for its distinctive leathery shell and strong swimming prowess. These turtles migrate over great distances and have a widespread distribution. In addition to habitat degradation and entanglement in fishing gear, leatherbacks are also threatened by climate change. The protection of leatherback turtles and safeguarding their survival in the oceans throughout the world depend heavily on conservation initiatives.
